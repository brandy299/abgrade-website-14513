The Science

What is Astrobiology?

Astrobiology is the study of the nature and extent of life in the Universe — an inherently interdisciplinary field connecting astronomy, chemistry, biology, geology, and beyond.

Overview

The Big Questions

The only known example of life in the Universe is that on Earth. We recognize this life as a complex state of matter evolving in our planet's dynamic environment. By studying life on Earth we are able to begin to extrapolate our findings to other worlds and critically evaluate whether such phenomena may have occurred there.

What Astrobiology Seeks to Understand

Astrobiology aims to understand the physicochemical conditions and geological requirements for the emergence of life — what elements and molecules can constitute life, what the environmental and physiological limits of life are, where in the cosmos it can be found, and how it might be recognised.

When inquiring into the nature and extent of life in the Universe, the questions vary depending on the discipline. An astronomer might search for habitable astrophysical objects, while a chemist might wonder what phenomena initially constitute a habitable body or environment. No question in astrobiology is isolated — all are posed within a broader context.

An Interdisciplinary Endeavour

The open questions that astrobiology addresses are inherently interdisciplinary and, as such, require collaboration across various fields of research. Astrobiology manifests itself as an umbrella field encompassing many disciplines, including astronomy, chemistry, biology, geology, engineering, law, history, social sciences and more.

The study of astrobiology is an attempt to broaden our understanding of life as we know it and to view life as a cosmic phenomenon. In so doing, astrobiology addresses a deep human fascination — and by no means a new one. It is an incredibly exciting time for the field, with more cross-disciplinary research than ever before.
